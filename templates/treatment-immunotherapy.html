What Is Immunotherapy

"We are not just controlling flare-ups. We are changing the way your immune system behaves."

Immunotherapy uses small, controlled doses of your specific allergens to build tolerance gradually. Instead of reacting intensely each season, your immune system becomes less reactive over time.

That is why allergy shots are often called the closest thing to a long-term allergy solution. For many patients, symptom burden drops substantially, medication use decreases, and daily life becomes less disrupted.

At Allergy & Asthma Care of Waco, treatment is customized to your exact trigger profile and monitored carefully from build-up through maintenance.

The Process

How Immunotherapy Works

Immunotherapy is intentionally gradual — your body needs time to adapt. Here's what the journey looks like from your first appointment through long-term maintenance.

01

Comprehensive Allergy Testing

We begin with a skin prick test to identify exactly what triggers your immune system — trees, grasses, weeds, mold, pet dander, dust mites, and more. This forms the foundation of your personalized treatment plan.

02

Your Custom Serum

Based on your results, we compound a serum tailored specifically to your sensitivities. Your formula is adjusted over time as your tolerance builds — no two patients receive the same treatment.

03

The Build-Up Phase

You'll receive injections once or twice a week, with the dose gradually increasing. Most patients move through build-up over 6 to 12 months. After each shot, you'll wait 20–30 minutes at our office — a standard precaution we take with every patient.

04

Reaching Maintenance

Once you've reached your target dose, visits drop to once or twice a month. This is when most patients start noticing meaningful improvement in their day-to-day symptoms.

05

Long-Term Results

After 3 to 5 years, many patients experience lasting relief — even after treatment ends. For some, that means no more daily medication. For others, symptoms that were once debilitating become easy to manage.

Why It Matters

There's nothing wrong with antihistamines or nasal sprays — they work, and they're often appropriate. But they need to be taken every day, which means the day you stop, your symptoms return. They don't change anything about how your immune system responds to allergens.

Immunotherapy is different. By gradually building tolerance over time, many patients reach a point where they no longer need daily medication — or where their symptoms are mild enough that occasional treatment handles the rest.

It's a longer commitment. But the outcome is fundamentally different from anything a pill or spray can offer.

Short-Term Control

Antihistamines & Sprays

Helpful for symptom control while actively used, but they do not change the underlying immune response to triggers.

Long-Term Change

Allergy Shots

Gradually desensitize your immune system so reactions become milder, with benefits that can continue well beyond treatment completion.

Who It Helps

Conditions It Can Help

Allergy shots are effective for a broad range of allergic conditions. If any of the following sound familiar, immunotherapy may be worth discussing at your appointment.

  • Seasonal hay fever & congestion
  • Chronic sneezing & runny nose
  • Itchy, watery eyes
  • Sinus pressure & headaches
  • Allergic asthma
  • Dust mite sensitivity
  • Pet dander reactions
  • Mold allergy symptoms
  • Insect venom allergy
  • Year-round indoor allergies
  • Cedar fever (Mountain Cedar)
  • Oak, elm, and grass pollen
Central Texas · Waco

Why Waco Makes Allergies Harder

Central Texas has one of the longest and most intense allergy seasons in the country. Between Mountain Cedar in winter, oak and elm in spring, grass through early summer, and ragweed in fall — there's rarely a clean window.

For many Waco residents, that means symptoms year-round. Immunotherapy is formulated around your specific local triggers — including the ones that only matter in Central Texas — making it one of the most practical long-term options for people who live and work here.

  • Mountain Cedar Dec – Mar High
  • Oak & Elm Mar – May High
  • Grass Pollen May – Jul High
  • Ragweed Aug – Nov High
  • Mold Spores Year-round Moderate
Common Questions

Questions We Hear Often

Starting a new treatment can feel uncertain. Here are straightforward answers to the questions we hear most often — no jargon, no overselling.

  • A full course typically runs 3 to 5 years. You'll start with a build-up phase — injections once or twice a week for 6 to 12 months — before moving to a monthly maintenance schedule. Most patients begin noticing improvement within the first year.
  • The shots are given with a very small needle — similar to a flu shot. Most patients describe a mild pinch. You may notice minor redness or swelling at the injection site, which is normal and fades within an hour. We ask all patients to wait 20–30 minutes after each shot as a standard precaution.
  • Many insurance plans — including most PPOs — cover allergy immunotherapy. Our staff will verify your coverage before you begin and give you a clear picture of expected costs. We never want insurance to be a surprise.
  • Some patients notice improvement during the first allergy season after starting. For most, meaningful symptom reduction becomes clear after reaching the maintenance phase — typically within the first year. The full benefit often continues to build over several years of treatment.
  • Yes — allergy shots are appropriate for most children aged 5 and older. Starting earlier can be beneficial, as it may reduce the risk of developing new allergies or asthma over time. We see children regularly and tailor treatment to their needs and comfort.
  • If you're already on a maintenance dose when you become pregnant, you can generally continue. We typically don't start new patients during pregnancy. Most medications don't interfere with immunotherapy, but bring a complete list to your consultation and we'll review it together.
